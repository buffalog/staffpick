# Your Rating

After care, patients (or their families) can rate their experience. Your rating reflects the quality of care you deliver and, over time, influences how you're prioritised for new cases.

## How patient ratings work

When a case wraps up, a short satisfaction survey can be sent to the patient. It asks a handful of simple questions about their experience with you. Responses are aggregated into a rolling average — recent feedback (the last 90 and 180 days) is tracked so your rating reflects how you're doing now, not just historically.

## What the survey looks like

The survey is brief by design — a small set of rating questions and an optional comment. It contains no clinical detail; it's about the patient's experience of working with you (professionalism, communication, reliability, overall satisfaction).

## How ratings affect matching

Matching is driven mainly by hard fit — discipline, distance, availability — and your rating is one of the quality signals layered on top. Consistently strong ratings help you surface higher in the ranked list for cases you fit, and they're part of what earns **preferred** status with a tenant, which adds a further ranking boost.

A single piece of feedback won't swing things; the system looks at your rolling averages and the number of responses, so the picture is stable and fair.

## Keeping your rating healthy

- Communicate clearly with patients and families.
- Show up when scheduled — reliability is what patients notice most.
- If a case isn't a fit, **decline** it rather than accept and under-deliver.

## Questions about a rating

If you believe a rating is inaccurate or unfair, raise it with the scheduling team — they can review the context.
